This practical continues our analysis of the dataset from the publication Proteomic and Metabolomic Characterization of COVID-19 Patient Sera. We work with the training cohort (Supplementary Table 3), which contains proteomic profiles for 31 COVID-19 patients (13 Severe and 18 Non-severe), measured across 791 protein features. The goal of this assignment is to build and evaluate a machine learning classifier to distinguish Severe from Non-severe patients based on their proteomic profiles, identify the most important protein features driving the classification, and validate the model on an independent test cohort (Supplementary Table 4, 10 patients).

> Note: In the paper the RF was trained both with proteins and metabolites. The task specifically said “based on proteomic data”, so we excluded metabolites. This can cause major differences in the models.

Caveats:

  1. We use different data cleaning and imputation than in the paper.
  2. We used median imputation rather than the methods used in the paper.
  3. We imputed and scaled in each data split rather than globally to prevent data leakage — the paper does not explicitly state this.
  4. We had to reduce certain RF parameters to keep the running time reasonable with our limit of 6 CPUs.

Results:

Confusion Matrix: Four false negatives and two false positives, against the one false positive in the paper. Accuracy: 0.8065 against 0.935 in the original paper — lower, but acceptable. mTRY: Rather smooth drop, meaning the model is not only relying on a few strong features but is actually learning a general pattern. AUC/ROC: AUC is 0.9103 against 0.957 in the original paper. Lower than the original but falls in the same relative range — meaning if we draw a random severe patient alongside a random non-severe, the model gives more “risk” to the severe patient 91% of the time. The ROC rises sharply, meaning as the decision threshold rises, sensitivity improves yet specificity does not drop significantly. The model can differentiate groups quite well by allocating risk correctly.

Conclusion: The deviations from the original could be justified by differences in imputation, data splitting, and lower parameters. With this in mind, all metrics of our model are relatively high. The model appears to more or less balance negative and positive predictions, does not seem to overfit or underfit, while accuracy, sensitivity, and somewhat specificity report accurate predictions. We have to keep in mind that N is low (31), so even small deviations can have a high impact on metrics.

Results: Overall, the model predicts quite well. Accuracy is low (60%) but several caveats apply. Unlike the original, we only used proteins. The training data had 753 features after filtering, but the test data has 742 — meaning the model cannot use some learned proteins. N is just 10, so even one or two random mismatches shifts accuracy by 10–20%. AUC is quite good at 0.79, meaning the model gives severe patients a higher “risk” than non-severe 79% of the time. Looking at the confusion matrix, the model produced more false positives than false negatives — meaning it currently overestimates severity. We chose an arbitrary standard 50% cut-off; raising it slightly could lower false positives without impacting false negatives. In fact, there is a way to mathematically derive the optimal threshold from the ROC curve:
